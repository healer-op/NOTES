1.1 Foundations
Definition & Overview of Information Security Risk Management
Definition (Stallings + NIST SP 800-39)

Risk = "A measure of the extent to which an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event, expressed as a function of (i) the adverse impacts that would arise if the event occurs, and (ii) the likelihood of occurrence." (NIST SP 800-30 Rev.1)

Information Security Risk Management (ISRM) = The ongoing process of identifying, assessing, treating, and monitoring risks to information assets in an organization, with the goal of reducing risk to a level acceptable to management.

Mnemonic — Risk Equation
"TVA × I = Risk"
Threat × Vulnerability × Asset value × Impact likelihood = Total Risk Exposure
  • Why ISRM matters: Organizations cannot eliminate all risks (zero risk = impossible). ISRM helps prioritize WHICH risks to fix first using limited resources.
  • Three pillars (CIA Triad): Confidentiality (no unauthorized read), Integrity (no unauthorized change), Availability (system reachable when needed).
  • Stallings' definition: "Risk Management is the process of identifying risk, assessing risk, and taking steps to reduce risk to an acceptable level."
  • NIST 3-Tier Approach (SP 800-39): Tier 1 = Organization level · Tier 2 = Mission/Business level · Tier 3 = Information System level.
Ref: Stallings Ch.3 NIST SP 800-39 NIST SP 800-30 Rev.1
1.2 Process
The Risk Management Process — Step-by-Step
Mnemonic — NIST RMF 7 Steps
"PCS-IAAM" → "Please Categorize Systems, Implement And Authorize Monitoring"
Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorize → Monitor
NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF) — Flowchart
Step 0: PREPARE (added in NIST 800-37 Rev.2, 2018)
  → Establish context, identify stakeholders, define risk tolerance
          ▼
Step 1: CATEGORIZE the system
  → Use FIPS 199: Low / Moderate / High impact for C, I, A
          ▼
Step 2: SELECT security controls from NIST 800-53
  → Tailor baseline controls to your specific risk profile
          ▼
Step 3: IMPLEMENT controls
  → Deploy firewalls, encryption, access control, training
          ▼
Step 4: ASSESS control effectiveness (NIST 800-53A)
  → Test, audit, penetration testing, code review
          ▼
Step 5: AUTHORIZE system to operate
  → Authorizing Official (AO) signs off based on residual risk

Step 6: MONITOR continuously
  → Ongoing logs, SIEM alerts, periodic reassessment

   [New threat or change → loop back to Categorize]
Stallings' Simpler 4-Step Cycle

1. Risk Assessment → 2. Risk Mitigation → 3. Risk Monitoring → 4. Risk Communication. Both Stallings and NIST agree: risk management is a continuous loop, never a one-time activity.

1.3 Identification
Risk Identification, Threat Identification & Asset Identification
Mnemonic — What to Identify First
"ATV — Assets, Threats, Vulnerabilities"
Always identify in order: Assets (what you own) → Threats (who/what attacks) → Vulnerabilities (weaknesses they exploit)
A. Identifying Information Assets
  • People assets: Employees, contractors, customers, vendors with system access.
  • Procedures: SOPs, IT policies, recovery plans, training docs.
  • Data assets: Databases, customer records, IP, source code, financial data, emails.
  • Software: OS, applications, custom code, security tools.
  • Hardware: Servers, laptops, mobile devices, network equipment.
  • Networking: Routers, firewalls, internet links, VPN tunnels.
B. Threat Identification (Stallings' 12 Categories)
  • 1. Acts of Human Error/Failure — Accidental: employee mistakes, accidental deletion.
  • 2. Compromises to Intellectual Property — Piracy, copyright violation.
  • 3. Deliberate Acts of Espionage — Spying for competitors/nation-states.
  • 4. Deliberate Acts of Information Extortion — Ransomware, blackmail.
  • 5. Deliberate Acts of Sabotage/Vandalism — Stuxnet, website defacement.
  • 6. Deliberate Acts of Theft — Stealing equipment or data.
  • 7. Deliberate Software Attacks — Viruses, worms, ransomware, trojans.
  • 8. Forces of Nature — Floods, earthquakes, fires.
  • 9. Deviations in QoS by Service Providers — ISP outage, cloud downtime.
  • 10. Technical Hardware Failures/Errors — Disk crash, RAM errors.
  • 11. Technical Software Failures/Errors — Bugs, buffer overflows.
  • 12. Technological Obsolescence — End-of-life unsupported systems.
Ref: Stallings Ch.1 "Threats" NIST SP 800-30 Appendix D
1.4 Assessment
Risk Assessment, Risk Determination & Evaluation
Definition

Risk Assessment = The process of identifying threats, vulnerabilities, and the likelihood × impact of each threat-vulnerability pair on each asset, resulting in a risk score.

Risk Determination Formulas (Stallings + NIST 800-30)
Qualitative Risk:
  Risk = Likelihood × Impact
  (Both rated as Low / Medium / High)

Quantitative Risk:
  SLE  = Asset Value × Exposure Factor
  ALE  = SLE × ARO
  Risk = ALE (annual financial loss expected)

Stallings' Detailed Formula:
  Risk = (Likelihood × Asset Value) − (% Risk mitigated by current controls)
       + Uncertainty factor

Example:
  Likelihood   = 0.5
  Asset Value  = ₹1,00,000
  Control      = 60% effective
  Residual     = 0.5 × ₹1,00,000 × (1 − 0.6) = ₹20,000
Risk Evaluation — Decision Phase

After determining the risk score, compare it to the organization's risk appetite. If Risk > Appetite → must treat. If Risk ≤ Appetite → can accept & document. NIST calls this "Risk Response Decision" (SP 800-39 §3.2).

Ref: NIST SP 800-30 Rev.1 Stallings Ch.3.3
1.5 Strategies
Risk Control Strategies + Selecting the Right Strategy
Mnemonic — 5 Strategies
"DTAMA — Don't Take A Massive Approach lightly"
Defense (avoidance) · Transference · Acceptance · Mitigation · Termination
  • 1. Defense / Avoidance: Apply safeguards (firewalls, encryption, MFA) to eliminate the risk. Best when: cost of control < risk impact.
  • 2. Transference: Shift risk to a third party (cyber insurance, outsource to MSSP, contractual indemnity). Best when: risk can be insured economically.
  • 3. Mitigation: Reduce damage when risk occurs — IRP, DRP, BCP. Best when: risk cannot be fully prevented.
  • 4. Acceptance: Knowingly take the risk because cost-of-fix > potential damage. Must be formally documented & signed.
  • 5. Termination: Stop the risky activity altogether (e.g., discontinue a vulnerable service).
How to SELECT the Right Strategy — Decision Tree

Q1: Can we eliminate it cheaply? → YES → Defense
Q2: Can someone else carry the risk for us? → YES → Transference
Q3: Can we reduce damage if it happens? → YES → Mitigation
Q4: Is the risk smaller than the cost to fix? → YES → Acceptance
Q5: Is the activity itself too risky? → YES → Termination

Ref: Stallings Ch.3.4 NIST SP 800-39 §2.4
1.6 Math
Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) for Risk Control
CBA Formula — Step-by-Step
Step 1: Calculate ALE BEFORE the control
  ALE_before = SLE × ARO

Step 2: Estimate ALE AFTER the control
  ALE_after  = New SLE × New ARO

Step 3: Compute CBA
  CBA = ALE_before − ALE_after − Annual Cost of Control

Decision Rule:
  If CBA > 0  → Implement the control (saves money)
  If CBA < 0  → Reject (control costs more than the risk)

Example — Firewall worth installing?
  ALE_before = ₹6,00,000  (frequent intrusions)
  ALE_after  = ₹1,00,000  (firewall blocks 83%)
  Cost       = ₹2,00,000  /year
  CBA = 6,00,000 − 1,00,000 − 2,00,000 = +₹3,00,000
  → Positive: INSTALL the firewall
Ref: Stallings Ch.3.5 NIST SP 800-30 Appendix G
1.7 Treatment
Information Security Risk Treatment (ISO 27005 + NIST view)
Definition

Risk Treatment = The phase of risk management where you actually execute the chosen strategy. ISO 27005 calls it the implementation step that follows risk evaluation.

  • Step 1: Develop Risk Treatment Plan (RTP) — owners, deadlines, budgets, KPIs.
  • Step 2: Implement controls (NIST 800-53 catalog: ~1000 controls in 20 families).
  • Step 3: Document residual risk and obtain management sign-off (Authorizing Official in NIST RMF).
  • Step 4: Communicate & train all stakeholders on new procedures.
  • Step 5: Monitor effectiveness continuously; reassess every 6–12 months.
Ref: ISO 27005 NIST SP 800-53 Rev.5 Stallings Ch.3.6

2.1 Governance
Information Security Governance
Definition (NIST SP 800-100)

Information Security Governance = The system by which an organization directs and controls its information security activities. It ensures that security strategy aligns with business strategy, risks are managed properly, and resources are used responsibly.

Mnemonic — 5 Governance Outcomes
"SAVOR governance like a fine wine"
Strategic alignment · Accountability · Value delivery · Operational risk mgmt · Resource management
  • Strategic Alignment: Security goals match business goals — security is a business enabler, not a blocker.
  • Risk Management: Risks are identified, prioritized, treated within risk appetite.
  • Resource Management: Budget, people, tools allocated efficiently.
  • Performance Measurement: KPIs track security effectiveness (e.g., mean time to detect MTTD).
  • Value Delivery: Security spend produces measurable business value.
Governance Structure Example

Board of Directors → Sets risk appetite. CEO → Accountable. CISO → Owns InfoSec strategy. IT Security Team → Implements. Internal Audit → Independent verification. All Employees → Daily compliance.

Ref: NIST SP 800-100 Ch.2 ISO 27014 Stallings Ch.14
2.2 ISMS
Information Security Management System (ISMS)
Definition (ISO/IEC 27001)

ISMS = A systematic approach consisting of policies, procedures, processes, and people that an organization uses to manage information security risks. It is the management system that sustains InfoSec governance over time.

Mnemonic — Deming/PDCA Cycle
"PDCA — Plan, Do, Check, Act"
ISO 27001 ISMS follows the Deming cycle: Plan ISMS → Do (implement) → Check (audit) → Act (improve)
  • PLAN: Define scope, set policies, conduct risk assessment, choose controls (ISO 27001 Annex A or NIST 800-53).
  • DO: Implement controls, train staff, deploy technologies.
  • CHECK: Internal audits, management reviews, monitoring & logging.
  • ACT: Corrective & preventive actions, continual improvement.
  • Outcome: Organization can pursue ISO 27001 certification — globally recognized proof of strong InfoSec.
Ref: ISO/IEC 27001:2022 NIST SP 800-53 (parallel framework)
2.3 Policies
Information Security Policy & EISP (Enterprise InfoSec Policy)
Mnemonic — EISP Components
"OSCAR runs the enterprise security"
Overview · Scope · Compliance · Authority & roles · References to sub-policies
Definition

An Information Security Policy is a formal document that defines management's intent, expectations, and rules for protecting information assets. The EISP is the highest-level policy — also called the master/security program policy.

  • Statement of Purpose: Why the policy exists (e.g., protect customer data, ensure compliance).
  • Scope: WHO and WHAT the policy applies to (all employees, all systems, contractors).
  • Authority & Responsibilities: CISO owns it, IT enforces, employees comply.
  • Compliance: Regulations adhered to (IT Act 2000, GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS).
  • References: Points to ISSPs (issue-specific) and SSPs (system-specific).
Ref: Stallings Ch.14.3 NIST SP 800-12 Rev.1 Ch.5
2.4 Policy Types
Issue-Specific (ISSP) vs System-Specific (SSP) Policies
Mnemonic
"ISSP = behavior · SSP = configuration"
ISSP tells people what they may/may not do. SSP tells systems exactly how they must be configured.
Issue-Specific Policy (ISSP)
  • Focus: a specific topic / behavior
  • Audience: all employees
  • Language: plain English
  • Author: HR / Legal / Mgmt
  • Examples: Email AUP, BYOD policy, Social media policy, Internet usage
System-Specific Policy (SSP)
  • Focus: a specific technology / system
  • Audience: sysadmins / engineers
  • Language: technical
  • Author: IT / Security team
  • Examples: Firewall ACL, password complexity rules, DB backup schedule, AWS IAM config

2.5 Hierarchy
Policies vs Standards vs Procedures vs Guidelines
The Documentation Hierarchy
POLICY     ← What & Why (mandatory, high-level)
   │       Example: "All sensitive data must be encrypted."
   ▼
STANDARDS  ← Specific rules to enforce policy (mandatory)
   │       Example: "Use AES-256 with TLS 1.3 minimum."
   ▼
PROCEDURES ← Step-by-step how-to (mandatory)
   │       Example: "1. Generate key in HSM. 2. Configure
   │                 OpenSSL. 3. Test with openssl s_client..."
   ▼
GUIDELINES ← Best-practice suggestions (optional)
           Example: "Recommended: rotate keys every 90 days."
  • Policy: What & why — mandatory, abstract.
  • Standard: Mandatory specific rules (e.g., must use AES-256).
  • Procedure: Mandatory step-by-step instructions.
  • Guideline: Recommended best practices — not mandatory.
Ref: Stallings Ch.14.3 NIST SP 800-12 Rev.1
2.6 Evaluation
Information Security Evaluation (Maturity Models & Audits)
  • Internal Audits: Periodic check by org's own audit team — verify ISMS works.
  • External Audits: Independent third-party assessment (e.g., for ISO 27001 certification).
  • Gap Analysis: Compare current state vs target framework (NIST CSF / ISO).
  • Penetration Testing: Ethical hackers try to break in — find vulnerabilities before attackers.
  • Maturity Models: CMMI, NIST CSF Tiers (Partial → Risk-Informed → Repeatable → Adaptive), C2M2.
  • Metrics & KPIs: MTTD, MTTR, # of incidents, % patching SLA met, training completion %.
NIST CSF 2.0 — 6 Functions for Evaluation

GOVERN (new in 2.0) → IDENTIFY → PROTECT → DETECT → RESPOND → RECOVER. Each function has Categories & Subcategories that can be evaluated against current implementation.

3.1 Foundations
Understanding Security Incident Response (IR)
Definitions (NIST SP 800-61 Rev.2)

Event = Any observable occurrence in a system (e.g., user logs in, file changes).
Adverse event = Event with negative consequences (system crash, malware infection).
Incident = A violation or imminent threat of violation of security policies / acceptable use.
Incident Response (IR) = The structured organizational process for handling incidents to limit damage, recover quickly, and learn from each event.

Why a Formal IR Program Matters

1. Reduces dwell time (time attacker stays in system). 2. Limits financial damage. 3. Required by law (GDPR demands 72-hour notification). 4. Preserves forensic evidence for prosecution. 5. Builds customer trust through transparent handling.

Ref: NIST SP 800-61 Rev.2 §1 Stallings Ch.17.1
3.2 Lifecycle
Life Cycle of Security Incident Response (NIST 4 Phases)
Mnemonic — NIST 800-61 Lifecycle
"PICERL — People In Crisis Easily Recover & Learn"
Preparation · Identification · Containment · Eradication · Recovery · Lessons learned (also known as PICERL or SANS 6-step)
NIST SP 800-61 Rev.2 — 4-Phase Cycle
PHASE 1: PREPARATION
  • Build IR team (CSIRT)
  • Create IR plan, policies, playbooks
  • Deploy tools (SIEM, EDR, forensics kit)
  • Train staff, run tabletop exercises
          ▼
PHASE 2: DETECTION & ANALYSIS
  • Monitor logs, SIEM alerts, IDS/IPS
  • Validate incident is real (not false positive)
  • Categorize: malware? insider? DDoS?
  • Determine scope & severity
          ▼
PHASE 3: CONTAINMENT, ERADICATION & RECOVERY
  • Containment: Isolate infected hosts (short-term)
  • Eradication: Remove malware, close vulnerabilities
  • Recovery: Restore from clean backups, validate
          ▼
PHASE 4: POST-INCIDENT ACTIVITY
  • Lessons learned meeting (within 2 weeks)
  • Update playbooks, controls, training
  • Legal & PR follow-up
  • Evidence retention for prosecution
          ▼
   [Loop back: improvements feed Phase 1]
Ref: NIST SP 800-61 Rev.2 §3 SANS PICERL Model
3.3 Phase 1
Preparation for Cyber Security Incidents
  • IR Policy & Plan: Approved by management, defines roles, escalation paths.
  • Playbooks: Step-by-step guides for common incidents (ransomware, phishing, DDoS).
  • Tools: SIEM (Splunk/QRadar), EDR (CrowdStrike), forensic suite (FTK, EnCase), packet capture (Wireshark).
  • Communication Plan: Who to call: legal, PR, law enforcement, regulators (CERT-In in India, 6-hour reporting rule).
  • Training & Drills: Tabletop exercises every quarter; full red-team annually.
  • Backups: 3-2-1 rule (3 copies, 2 media, 1 offsite); test restorations monthly.
Ref: NIST SP 800-61 §3.1 CERT-In Directive 2022 (India)
3.4 Phase 2 & 3
Responding to a Cyber Security Incident
Mnemonic — Containment Strategy
"SCRAMble — Stop, Capture, Restore, Analyze, Mitigate"
When an incident hits: Stop the spread → Capture forensic evidence → Restore from clean state → Analyze root cause → Mitigate future risk
  • Detection sources: SIEM alerts, IDS/IPS, antivirus, user reports ("I clicked a weird link"), threat intel feeds.
  • Triage: Validate incident, assign severity (Critical/High/Medium/Low).
  • Short-term containment: Disconnect from network, block malicious IPs, disable accounts.
  • Forensic capture: Memory dump, disk image — preserve evidence chain of custody.
  • Long-term containment: Apply patches, reset all credentials, deploy new controls.
  • Eradication: Remove malware, close vulnerabilities, rebuild compromised systems.
  • Recovery: Restore from verified clean backups; monitor closely for re-infection.
Ref: NIST SP 800-61 §3.2-3.3 Stallings Ch.17.4
3.5 Phase 4
Cyber Security Incident Follow-Up (Post-Incident Activity)
  • Lessons-Learned Meeting: Held within 2 weeks; ALL stakeholders attend (IR team, mgmt, IT, legal).
  • Key questions: Exactly what happened? When was it detected? How fast was response? What worked? What failed?
  • Update artifacts: Playbooks, IR plan, controls, training material based on lessons.
  • Regulatory reporting: File required notifications (GDPR 72hrs, CERT-In 6hrs, RBI for banks).
  • Evidence retention: Keep logs & forensic data for 1–7 years for legal action.
  • Metrics: MTTD (mean time to detect), MTTR (mean time to respond), $ damage, # systems affected.
Ref: NIST SP 800-61 §3.4
3.6 Team
Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT)
Definition

A CSIRT is a designated team of skilled professionals responsible for receiving, reviewing, and responding to computer security incidents. Also called CERT (Computer Emergency Response Team) or SOC (Security Operations Center) for 24×7 ops.

Mnemonic — CSIRT Roles
"TIMID HF — Team has Incident Manager, Investigator, Decision-maker, Hunter, Forensicator"
CSIRT = mix of leadership, technical, legal, and communications experts
  • Incident Manager / IR Lead: Coordinates response, makes decisions.
  • Security Analysts (Tier 1, 2, 3): Triage alerts, deep investigation, threat hunting.
  • Forensic Investigator: Captures evidence, performs malware analysis.
  • Threat Intelligence Analyst: Correlates with known TTPs (MITRE ATT&CK).
  • Legal Counsel: Compliance, breach notification laws.
  • PR / Communications: Internal & external messaging.
  • Mgmt Liaison: Reports to executives, board.
CSIRT Models

Central CSIRT: One team for whole org. Distributed CSIRT: Multiple regional teams. Coordinating CSIRT: Between many orgs (e.g., CERT-In, US-CERT). Outsourced/MSSP: Hired third-party 24×7 SOC.

Ref: NIST SP 800-61 §2.4 CERT-In (India) FIRST.org
3.7 Architecture
Design of Security Architecture
Definition

Security Architecture = The structured design of security controls, technologies, and policies arranged in layers across an organization's IT environment. It implements the Defense in Depth principle.

Mnemonic — Defense Layers
"PNESH-DA — Physical, Network, Endpoint, System, Human, Data & Application"
7 protective layers from outside in: Physical → Perimeter (Network) → Endpoint → System (OS) → Human (training) → Data → Application
  • 1. Physical Layer: Locks, biometrics, CCTV, guards.
  • 2. Network/Perimeter: Firewalls, IDS/IPS, VPN, WAF.
  • 3. Endpoint: Antivirus, EDR, host firewall, MDM.
  • 4. System: OS hardening, patches, least privilege.
  • 5. Application: Secure coding, code reviews, WAF, OWASP Top 10 mitigation.
  • 6. Data: Encryption at rest & in transit, DLP, tokenization.
  • 7. Human: Awareness training, phishing simulations.
Modern Architectures (2024+)

Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) — NIST SP 800-207. "Never trust, always verify." Every request authenticated. SASE (Secure Access Service Edge) — combines SD-WAN + security in cloud. SOAR — Security Orchestration, Automation & Response.

Ref: NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust) Stallings Ch.16 SABSA Framework

Risk Management in Financial Processing
Mnemonic — 5 Financial Risks
"COMRO — Credit, Operational, Market, Reputational, Outage"
5 main risks for banks & payment processors

  • Cyber Risk: Hacks, fraud, data breach. Mitigation: NIST CSF, RBI Cybersecurity Framework 2016/2021.
  • Compliance/Regulatory Risk: Failing AML, KYC, PCI-DSS rules. Mitigation: dedicated compliance team.
  • Frameworks used: Basel III (banks), SOX (US listed cos), RBI Master Directions (India), PCI-DSS for cards.

4.2 PMBOK
Risk Management Process in Project Management (PMBOK)
Mnemonic — PMBOK 6 Steps
"PIQ-QPM — Plan, Identify, Qualitative, Quantitative, Plan responses, Monitor"
PMI's 6-process risk management area
  • 1. Plan Risk Management: Define methodology, roles, budget. Output: Risk Management Plan.
  • 2. Identify Risks: Brainstorming, SWOT, expert interviews, checklists. Output: Risk Register.
  • 3. Qualitative Analysis: Probability × Impact matrix. Quick prioritization (H/M/L).
  • 4. Quantitative Analysis: Numerical analysis — Monte Carlo, EMV (Expected Monetary Value), decision trees.
  • 5. Plan Risk Responses: Avoid · Transfer · Mitigate · Accept (positive risks: Exploit · Share · Enhance · Accept).
  • 6. Monitor & Control: Track risks throughout project; adjust as new risks appear.
Ref: PMBOK 7th Edition (PMI) ISO 31000
4.3 ERM
Risk Management Process in Business (Enterprise Risk Mgmt)
Definition (COSO ERM Framework)

Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) = An integrated approach to identifying and managing all risks across the entire organization — strategic, operational, financial, compliance, reputational. Focused on creating, preserving, and realizing value.

  • Strategic risks: New competitors, disruption (Uber to taxi industry).
  • Operational risks: Supply chain breakdown, staff turnover.
  • Financial risks: Cash flow, currency fluctuations.
  • Compliance risks: GDPR fines, lawsuits.
  • Reputational risks: Bad PR, social media crisis.
  • Frameworks: COSO ERM (2017), ISO 31000:2018, NIST CSF (cyber subset).

Risk Management in Software Engineering (Boehm's Model)
Mnemonic — Boehm's 6 Steps
"IAPP-RMR — Identify, Analyze, Prioritize, Plan, Resolve, Monitor, Review"
Barry Boehm's classic software risk management model (1991)

4.6 Case Study
Case Study: BHIM App & Google Pay — Risk Analysis
Background

BHIM (Bharat Interface for Money) launched by NPCI in 2016. Google Pay (formerly Tez, 2017). Both run on UPI. In May 2020, vpnMentor researchers discovered a misconfigured AWS S3 bucket linked to BHIM exposing 7.26 million users' Aadhaar, PAN, UPI IDs, and bank info.

  • Risk 1 — Cloud Misconfiguration (BHIM): S3 bucket left public. NIST CSF: PROTECT-PT-PS failure. Fix: CSPM tools, automated audits, principle of least privilege.
  • Risk 2 — Identity Theft / Data Privacy: Aadhaar exposure → KYC fraud, fake loans. Fix: data minimization, tokenization, encryption at rest (AES-256).
  • Risk 3 — Phishing & Fake Apps: Fake "BHIM" apps on Play Store steal UPI PIN. Fix: NPCI app whitelisting, certificate pinning, user education.
  • Risk 4 — SIM Swap / OTP Interception: Attacker hijacks phone number → catches OTPs. Fix: device binding, biometric auth, anomaly-based fraud detection.
  • Risk 5 — Social Engineering ("Request Money" scams): Scammer sends collect requests pretending to "send" money. Fix: UI redesign, education campaigns, transaction limits.
Google Pay's Architecture Advantage

GPay uses tokenization (real card numbers never stored), device binding, Google's ML-driven anomaly detection, and Google Play Protect. This gives it a stronger risk posture than BHIM, which had the 2020 leak.

NIST CSF 2.0 Mapping

GOVERN: NPCI sets policy. IDENTIFY: Map UPI IDs, servers, Aadhaar links. PROTECT: AES-256, MFA, tokenization. DETECT: Real-time anomaly alerts (location/amount). RESPOND: Freeze accounts, NPCI dispute resolution. RECOVER: Refund framework, user notification within 24 hrs.

Ref: vpnMentor BHIM Report 2020 NPCI UPI Procedural Guidelines RBI Master Direction on Digital Payments

5.3 GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
Definition

GDPR = European Union Regulation (EU) 2016/679, effective 25 May 2018. The world's most strict and comprehensive data privacy law. Applies to any organization globally that processes personal data of EU residents (extra-territorial).

Mnemonic — GDPR's 7 Principles
"LIST-DAI — Lawfulness, Integrity, Storage, Transparency, Data minimization, Accuracy, Accountability"
Article 5 — the 7 fundamental principles of GDPR
  • 1. Lawfulness, Fairness, Transparency: Have legal basis (consent, contract, legitimate interest); be honest with users.
  • 2. Purpose Limitation: Only use data for the specific stated purpose.
  • 3. Data Minimization: Collect only what is strictly necessary.
  • 4. Accuracy: Keep data accurate; correct mistakes promptly.
  • 5. Storage Limitation: Don't keep data longer than needed.
  • 6. Integrity & Confidentiality: Protect data with encryption, access control.
  • 7. Accountability: Can demonstrate compliance with all of the above.
8 Data Subject Rights (Articles 12–22)

1. Right to be informed · 2. Right of access · 3. Right to rectification · 4. Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") · 5. Right to restrict processing · 6. Right to data portability · 7. Right to object · 8. Rights related to automated decision-making.

GDPR Penalties

Up to €20 million OR 4% of global annual turnover — whichever is HIGHER. Examples: Amazon fined €746M (2021), Meta fined €1.2 billion (2023), Google fined €50M (2019). Breach notification within 72 hours to the Data Protection Authority.

Key Roles

Data Controller: Decides why & how data is processed. Data Processor: Processes on controller's behalf. DPO (Data Protection Officer): Mandatory for large-scale or sensitive data processing. Supervisory Authority: National regulator (e.g., CNIL in France, ICO in UK).

Ref: EU Regulation 2016/679 GDPR.eu India DPDP Act 2023 (similar)

NIST SP 800-171 — Protecting CUI in Non-Federal Systems
Definition

NIST SP 800-171 is a streamlined set of 110 security requirements across 14 families designed to protect Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) when handled by non-federal organizations (contractors, subcontractors, universities). Mandated by DFARS 252.204-7012 for all DoD contractors.

What is CUI?

Controlled Unclassified Information = Sensitive but not classified federal data. Examples: technical specs of defense systems, export-controlled data, financial records, certain PII. Not "Top Secret" but if leaked, harms national interest.

Bonus Topics — Cookies, GDPR Compliance & UPI

What this section covers

Three topics frequently asked in vivas and short-question sections: Types of Cookies (web security), GDPR Compliance (how to actually achieve it), and UPI (Unified Payments Interface — India's payment backbone).

B.1 Web Security
Types of Cookies — Complete Guide
Definition

An HTTP Cookie is a small piece of data (usually <4KB) that a website stores on the user's browser. The browser sends it back with every subsequent request to that site. Used for sessions, preferences, tracking, authentication.

Mnemonic — 8 Types of Cookies
"SP-FT-SS-HZ — Session/Persistent, First/Third, Secure/SameSite, HttpOnly/Zombie"
Categorized 4 ways: by lifetime, by origin, by security flags, by special types
Category 1 — By Lifetime
  • 1. Session Cookies: Stored only in RAM. Deleted when browser closes. Used for shopping carts, login sessions. Most secure for sessions.
  • 2. Persistent Cookies: Stored on disk with expiry date (days–years). Used for "remember me", language preferences, ad tracking.
Category 2 — By Origin
  • 3. First-Party Cookies: Set by the website you're visiting (e.g., amazon.com sets a cookie when you visit Amazon). Generally safer.
  • 4. Third-Party Cookies: Set by another domain embedded on the site (e.g., DoubleClick ads on amazon.com). Used heavily for cross-site tracking. Being phased out by Chrome (2024–25), already blocked by Safari, Firefox.
Category 3 — By Security Flags
  • 5. Secure Cookies: Only sent over HTTPS. Set with Secure flag. Prevents eavesdropping.
  • 6. HttpOnly Cookies: Cannot be accessed by JavaScript (document.cookie). Set with HttpOnly flag. Prevents XSS attacks from stealing session cookies.
  • 7. SameSite Cookies: Restrict cookie sending in cross-site requests. Three values: Strict (never cross-site) · Lax (default in modern browsers) · None (must also be Secure). Prevents CSRF attacks.
Category 4 — Special Types
  • 8. Zombie Cookies / Supercookies: Cookies that recreate themselves after deletion using HTML5 LocalStorage, Flash LSOs, or ETags. Privacy violation — banned in many jurisdictions.
  • 9. Authentication Cookies: Specifically used for login state. Should be Secure + HttpOnly + SameSite=Strict.
  • 10. Tracking/Analytics Cookies: Google Analytics _ga, Facebook _fbp. Need user consent under GDPR.
Example — A Secure Cookie
Set-Cookie: sessionId=abc123;
            Secure;        ← only sent over HTTPS
            HttpOnly;      ← JS cannot read it
            SameSite=Strict; ← no cross-site
            Max-Age=3600;       ← expires in 1 hour
            Path=/;
            Domain=bank.com
Cookie-Related Attacks

Session Hijacking: Steal session cookie → impersonate user. Mitigated by Secure + HttpOnly. CSRF (Cross-Site Request Forgery): Trick browser into sending cookie with malicious request. Mitigated by SameSite + CSRF tokens. XSS (Cross-Site Scripting): Inject JS to read cookies. Mitigated by HttpOnly + Content Security Policy (CSP).

GDPR & Cookies — The Cookie Banner Law

Under GDPR + ePrivacy Directive, any non-essential cookie (analytics, ads, tracking) requires explicit, informed, opt-in consent from users BEFORE setting. That's why every EU website shows a "Accept Cookies" banner. Essential cookies (session, login) don't need consent.

Ref: RFC 6265 (HTTP State Mgmt) OWASP Session Management EU ePrivacy Directive
B.2 Privacy Law
GDPR Compliance — How to Actually Comply
Mnemonic — 10 Steps to GDPR Compliance
"DAMP-CRIBED — Document, Audit, Map, Privacy, Consent, Rights, Incident, Breach, Encrypt, DPO"
A practical 10-step compliance roadmap
  • Step 1 — Awareness: Train all employees and management. GDPR is everyone's responsibility.
  • Step 2 — Data Audit / Mapping: Document WHAT personal data you collect, WHY, WHERE it's stored, WHO has access, HOW LONG you keep it. Output: Record of Processing Activities (RoPA), mandatory under Article 30.
  • Step 3 — Lawful Basis: For every data use, identify a legal basis from Art. 6: (a) Consent (b) Contract (c) Legal obligation (d) Vital interests (e) Public task (f) Legitimate interest.
  • Step 4 — Update Privacy Notice: Plain language. Tell users what data, why, how long, who you share with, their rights, contact details of DPO.
  • Step 5 — Consent Mechanism: Where consent is the legal basis, must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous. Pre-ticked checkboxes are NOT valid. Easy to withdraw.
  • Step 6 — Data Subject Rights: Build processes to handle the 8 rights (access, rectification, erasure, etc.) within 30 days.
  • Step 7 — Privacy by Design (Art. 25): Bake privacy into systems from day 1 — not bolted on later. Use data minimization, encryption, pseudonymization.
  • Step 8 — DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment): Mandatory for high-risk processing (large-scale profiling, biometrics, etc.).
  • Step 9 — Breach Notification: Notify supervisory authority within 72 hours. Notify affected users without undue delay if high risk.
  • Step 10 — Appoint a DPO: Mandatory for: public bodies, large-scale monitoring, large-scale special category data. The DPO reports independently to senior management.
Technical & Organizational Measures (TOMs) — Article 32

Technical: Encryption (AES-256) at rest & in transit, pseudonymization, access control, MFA, backups, intrusion detection.
Organizational: Policies, training, NDAs, vendor due diligence, breach response plan, regular audits, DPO oversight.

Cross-Border Transfers (Critical!)

Sending EU data outside EU is restricted. Allowed only via: Adequacy decisions (countries deemed safe — UK, Switzerland, Japan, but NOT India yet). Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) — most common. Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs) — for multinationals. After Schrems II ruling (2020), US transfers need extra safeguards.

India's DPDP Act 2023 — GDPR's Cousin

India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 mirrors GDPR but is simpler. Key differences: No "right to be forgotten" (only erasure on consent withdrawal), no DPO concept (Significant Data Fiduciary instead), penalties up to ₹250 crore, child consent threshold = 18 (vs 16 in GDPR). DPDP Rules notified Jan 2025.

Ref: EU Reg 2016/679 (GDPR) DPDP Act 2023 (India) ICO Compliance Guide
B.3 Payments
UPI — Unified Payments Interface (Complete Guide)
Definition

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) = An instant real-time payment system developed by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India), launched 11 April 2016. Allows instant fund transfers between any two bank accounts using just a Virtual Payment Address (VPA / UPI ID), mobile number, QR code, or Aadhaar.

Mnemonic — UPI Components
"4 Pillars: PSP-PSP-Bank-Bank"
Payer's PSP App ↔ Payee's PSP App ↔ via NPCI UPI Switch ↔ between Payer's Bank and Payee's Bank
UPI Transaction Flow
User A wants to pay ₹500 to User B

1. User A opens GPay (PSP app) → enters User B's UPI ID
2. Enters ₹500 → enters 4/6-digit UPI PIN
3. GPay → encrypts request → sends to NPCI UPI Switch
4. NPCI verifies User A's PIN with User A's Bank
5. User A's Bank debits ₹500
6. NPCI routes credit to User B's Bank
7. User B's Bank credits ₹500 → confirms back via NPCI
8. Both User A & User B see SMS/notification — all in 2-5 seconds!
Key Features
  • 24×7×365: Works any time including holidays — unlike NEFT/RTGS earlier.
  • Single Click 2-Factor Auth: Device binding (phone bound to bank account) + UPI PIN.
  • Virtual Payment Address (VPA): abc@oksbi or 9876543210@ybl — no need to share account number.
  • Push & Pull: Send money OR request money (collect request).
  • Limits: Up to ₹1 lakh per transaction (₹2L for capital markets, education, healthcare). 20 txns per day.
  • Free for users: No charges (zero MDR for P2P/P2M up to ₹2,000).
  • Volume: 14+ billion transactions/month (2024) — world's largest real-time payment system.
UPI Security Architecture

1. Device Binding: UPI app bound to one specific phone (SIM + IMEI). New phone = re-register.
2. UPI PIN: Set inside app via debit card; only user knows it. Encrypted at NPCI level.
3. Tokenization: Real account number never shared with merchants. Tokens used.
4. End-to-end encryption: Every UPI message encrypted using AES-256.
5. NPCI Fraud Risk Management (FRM): ML-based real-time anomaly detection.
6. Two-factor by design: Possession (phone) + Knowledge (PIN).

UPI Variants & Innovations
  • UPI 2.0 (2018): Linked overdraft accounts, signed intent, invoice in inbox.
  • UPI Lite (2022): Wallet-style for small amounts (≤₹500), no PIN required, faster.
  • UPI on Credit Cards (RuPay): Pay via credit card linked to UPI (2023).
  • UPI International: UAE, Singapore, France, Sri Lanka, Mauritius, Bhutan, Nepal — and growing.
  • UPI AutoPay: Recurring mandates (Netflix subscriptions, SIPs).
  • UPI 123Pay (2022): Feature phone UPI — works without internet via IVR/missed call.
  • UPI Circle / Family UPI (2024): Allow trusted users to pay from your account with limits.
Common UPI Scams & How to Avoid

1. "Collect Request" Scam: Scammer sends money REQUEST disguised as "I'm sending you money." Victim approves & loses money. → Never enter UPI PIN to RECEIVE money.
2. Fake Customer Support: Fraudsters pose as bank/Paytm support, install AnyDesk, take over phone. → Banks never ask for PIN/OTP.
3. QR Code Scam: Scanning a malicious QR sends YOUR money. → Scanning + entering PIN = paying, not receiving.
4. SIM Swap: Attacker hijacks your phone number. → Use UPI Lite for small txns; enable extra bank alerts.

UPI Ecosystem Players

NPCI: Operates UPI switch (the central rail). Banks: ~500+ on UPI; provide accounts. PSP Apps: User-facing apps — Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm, BHIM, WhatsApp Pay, CRED, Amazon Pay (top 7 = 90%+ market share). RBI: Regulator. NPCI Bharat BillPay: For utility bills.

Risk Management View on UPI

UPI's risk management aligns with NIST CSF: IDENTIFY (device binding maps users to phones) → PROTECT (encryption, tokenization, 2FA) → DETECT (FRM ML model, anomaly detection) → RESPOND (Auto-block suspicious accounts, dispute resolution within T+1) → RECOVER (Refunds via NPCI's Online Dispute Resolution).

Ref: NPCI UPI Procedural Guidelines RBI Master Direction on Digital Payment Security CERT-In UPI Advisories
